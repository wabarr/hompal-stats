Hmm, Papio sp. has a much larger variance than the other groups. This is likely due to the inclusion of two species (P. anubis and P. hamadryas) within the category. The authors were more interested in comparing the modern baboons to fossil baboons, not distinguishing between the modern groups. So we can let this fly

Perform the ANOVA

This can be done utilizing anova() or aov(), but you may prefer one over the other depending on what information you need.

What do the help pages tell us?

?anova
?aov

So, the purpose of anova() is simply to generate ANOVA tables for a fitted model, while while aov() performs the actual analysis of variance (hence their names).

Getting an ANOVA table using anova(lm())

baboonanova <- anova(lm(Delta.C13 ~ Taxon, data = baboondiet))

The p-value suggests that we can reject the null hypothesis, indicating that there is a significant, non-random difference between our 6 group means.

But exactly which groups actually differ from one another?

This can be tested with with Tukeys HSD test, for which you must use your saved aov() results, NOT the results from anova(lm()).

Tukeys HSD test - Mind your ps:

T. brumpti and East African T. oswaldi differ from each other, East African T. oswaldi and P. boisei are statistically indifferent, and South African T. oswaldi is statistically the same to all of these taxa.

13C values for modern baboons are significantally different from those of fossil baboons and hominins, though there is some overlap with P. robustus.
